Consider the following tables for Morse Code:

  1. The "Header" section of the table has a lightblue background
  2. The "Data" section of the table has a lighyellow background
  3. Notice Bit #1 data in all 4 tables. Its two values, "dot" and "dash" vertically repeat themselves over and over!
  4. Now look at Bit #2. Its two values, "dot" and "dash" also repeat vertically but last for both values of Bit #1.
  5. Bit #3 and Bit #4 repeat in the same fashion of Bit #2, where
    Bit #3 allows Bit #2 to cycle through its two values
    Bit #4 allows Bit #3 to cycle its two values
  6. Did you notice the "blank" spaces in the data for Bit #2, Bit # 3, and Bit #4.! It was Samuel Morse's choice to assign "letters" different lengths.
    1. "E" & "T" only need 1 Bit (eg 1-bit words)
    2. "I", "A", "N", & "M" need 2 Bits (2-bit words)
    3. "S", "U", "R", "W", "D", "K", "G" & "O" are all 3-bit words
    4. Finally the remaining 16 letters are 4-bit words

Notice: Samuel Morse chose to use "varying" word lengths(1-bit,2-bits,3-bits,4-bits). It served his purpose well. The Table above and the Key to the right present the same information. Is one better than the other? Regardless the Table on the left is in the form that is used in "digital" electronics. It is usually called a "Truth Table" so switch over to "Truth Tables" to learn more about them.

In Examples you saw what a "Truth Table" looks like. It is composed of a "Header" and "Body". The "Header" usually has a Title and the words "Inputs" and "Outputs" , which require that all inputs(words) must be the same length. With this in mind, we'll pick a word length of 4(eg 4-bits) For Input values, we'll use "0" & "1" in place of "dot" & "dash", which is commonly done. We are not interested in any particular value for the Outputs, so we'll fill it with a "-".
